Figure 3

Smoothing out Time Series Data

Historical data suggests that search trends for unemployment benefits often precede official changes in the unemployment rate. This is because individuals who are experiencing job loss or uncertainty tend to search for information on unemployment benefits before the official statistics are released. In fact, research has shown that Google search data can be used as a leading indicator of unemployment claims, with search volume often increasing before official unemployment rates rise. This is because search data can capture real-time sentiment and concerns of individuals, providing an early warning signal of labor market stress.

Smoothing out Time Series Data

Unemployment claims in the US skyrocketed in March 2020, reaching a record high of 6.9 million initial claims, as the COVID-19 pandemic led to widespread lockdowns, business closures, and unprecedented job losses across various industries. This spike was largely driven by the sudden and drastic measures taken to contain the pandemic, which resulted in a sharp contraction in economic activity and a massive displacement of workers. Notably, while Google searches for unemployment benefits often precede official rate increases, the 2008 economic crisis saw a sharp jump in the official unemployment rate to 10% in a short time, exceeding any increase in Google searches. This highlights the severity of the crisis and the limits of using search data as a leading indicator.
